Streamlining Physician Oversight

Overview

TrialSpark is a biotech company whose mission is to bring treatment to patients faster by solving some of the biggest operational challenges of clinical trials, from recruiting subjects to standardizing the collection of data.

My team, known as Trial Operations (TrialOps), worked on the company’s proprietary software called Pilot used primarily by research coordinators to capture human subject data throughout a study. This data eventually gets sent to the FDA for final review.

Role & Duration

  • Designer: Research, wireframing, testing & prototyping
  • Squad: 5 engineers, 2 designers, 1 PM
  • 12 weeks: Feb - May 2020

Industry Definitions

Clinical Research Coordinator (CRC): someone with a research/medical background who facilitates and coordinates daily clinical trial activities. CRCs are responsible for conducting patient visits in accordance with the study protocol and managing the collection of patient data throughout a study.

Primary Investigator (PI): a physician/MD who supervises the conduct of a study at a given site. PIs are responsible for overseeing patient visits, ensuring the safety and well-being of the study participant. They provide clinical judgment when necessary and sign off on paper and electronic forms where subject data is captured.

Problem Framing

The FDA requires Principal Investigators (PIs) to sign off on forms that capture human data. Yet, TrialSpark PIs aren’t easily able to navigate to forms that require signoff without the help of coordinators. This is problematic for two reasons:

1. CRCs are blocked without these signatures, causing them to wait unnecessarily long hours after a patient visit to physically meet with PIs.

2. Remote patient visits are becoming more common, which means PIs and CRCs need to be able to execute all study tasks autonomously.


Context

When I started at TrialSpark, physicians were not accustomed to using Pilot software independently. Instead, they relied on research coordinators to help them sign in and navigate to the appropriate forms that required action. Pilot, at the time, was simply not built to support physicians’ workflows. For example, there were no visual indicators from the homepage directing physicians to the specific forms that required their review. Moreover, the system lacked simple microinteractions, like tooltips and progress indicators, to make it easier for users to understand where to go and anticipate what will happen next.

Current: no signature status indicators exist, so user has to check each subject and subsequent form individually to identify whether a form is signed or needs a signature.


Proposed: signature status indicators on each page help users navigate to the appropriate forms that require signatures.

Obviously this was inefficient. Coordinators had to repeatedly wait long hours (5+) after a patient visit to meet with physicians to get signatures. This was especially inconvenient because TrialSpark coordinators were not permanently stationed at these sites, often having to travel to multiple sites in a day. Though securing signatures at the end of a patient visit was the ideal scenario, this was rarely feasible.

Then COVID-19 happened,

which sparked a team-wide initiative to support and streamline remote signoff.


How might we support and streamline remote signoff without compromising patient safety and data integrity?

Research & Synthesis

If my team was to improve the electronic signature flow, we needed to understand the regulations around PI oversight: Why are signatures necessary? To what extent can we change the status quo?

Regulation vs Guidelines

There is a lot of ambiguity around the interpretation of Good Clinical Practice (GCP). When it comes to physician oversight, the FDA establishes general guidelines that can be translated broadly and (too often) inconsistently.

This became apparent to me when interviewing CRCs who had varied experiences working with auditors and data monitors at the site level. I quickly discovered that varying degrees of leniency ultimately affect how coordinators approach physician oversight.

For example, some CRCs held very conservative views on PI oversight (e.g. it’s necessary that my PI looks over and signs off on all forms in a timely manner to ensure accuracy and proper oversight, especially if an auditor were to investigate), while others seemed more lax (e.g. unless there’s an urgent matter, I don’t think it’s necessary for my PI to sign off on every form).

As a company, we constantly have to weigh the potential benefits and risks of efficiency over thoroughness.

Unless it’s an urgent matter, I don’t think it’s necessary for my PI to sign off on every form.

User Pain Points

Whenever I spoke with CRCs, I was reminded just how frustrating it was to track down PIs for signatures.

One time I waited like 5 hours just to get one last signature. It was really frustrating. - CRC

I literally have to quadruple check [Pilot] to ensure that all forms have been signed off. This can mean clicking into 20 forms sometimes... There’s no way around it. - CRC

I don’t trust that my PI can navigate through Source without accidentally modifying data. I feel better helping him in-person so that I can answer any questions. My PI is smart, obviously, but not the most tech-savvy. - CRC


Solving their frustrations became my underlying motivation.

#1 New UI System

Our focus on refining PI Oversight coincided with the rollout of our new UI system. This meant that any new feature would need to fit into both the current and redesigned experience. I worked closely with the lead designer for the new UI system to help shape component decisions (e.g. new navigation, status labels, popovers, copy, primary and secondary button interactions, form construction etc).

#2 Multiple User Types

Even though my focus was on the experience of CRCs and PIs, I needed to understand the impact of feature changes on all 4 user types, including auditors (CRAs) and clinical data managers (CDMs). Given that each user type has distinct permissions and views, it was important to specify whether or not each user would see/interact with the new changes.

#3 FDA Standards

Any changes to the actual signature flow needed to be vetted by our internal QA/audit team to ensure we were complying with FDA standards. For example, the FDA requires that PIs re-enter their credentials after each signature.

Learnings & Reflection

After each design sprint, my team liked to debrief with an exercise called “I like, I wish, I wonder…” to gather open and honest feedback. Here are my takeaways:

I like that design and engineering collaborated closely and efficiently so that we could incorporate the new UI system into the feature rollout. I also like that we were able to involve CRCs in the design process, ensuring that we were addressing their pain points head on.

I wish that we could have challenged the status quo by making more meaningful changes to the signature flow (e.g. batch signoff, sign via mobile instead of desktop, eliminating signatures altogether). However, this would have required much more time and cross-functional effort to align with clinical study team stakeholders and could have risked negatively impacting our relationship with conservative pharmaceutical companies (trial sponsors).

I wonder how long it would have taken us to prioritize these changes if COVID-19 had not impacted our ability to conduct in-person studies. Though CRC & PI user pain points had existed for a long time, there wasn’t a strong enough business reason to divert engineering resources towards addressing them. However, given how quickly the world around us can change the needs of a business and it’s users, it is even more important to be able to adapt quickly.
